Part Two — Within the Blocs
Canada

Socialism with a Human Face

How Canada used the game it invented to defy American dominance, forge an independent relationship with the Soviet Union, and discover unexpected respect for the enemy.

Argument Though Canada was less hostile towards the US than the Czechoslovaks were towards the Soviets, hockey was a major source of tension between the two countries — and also a tool for reconciliation. Canada competed aggressively against the Soviet Union not out of obligation to the US's ideological position, but for national pride, while also fostering independent diplomatic relations.
Hockey and Canadian Identity

The Game as National Expression

While the "birthplace" of hockey is disputed, the organized sport as it is known today was developed in Canada. Canadian hockey developed in the 19th century as a combination of indigenous lacrosse — known as baggataway — and stick-and-ball games from the British Isles, specifically Irish hurling, Scottish shinty, and English bandy.

The First Nations' idea of masculinity, which emphasized physicality and stoicism, was reflected in their style of play. Early settlers hoped to create a new Canadian masculine identity by bringing the speed and aggression of baggataway to hockey. As hockey spread internationally, it was known primarily for being violent and being Canadian — serving as both a national expression of masculinity and a way to resist American and British hegemony.

Canada-US Relations

The NHL, American Expansion, and Growing Resentment

The NHL was founded in 1917 with four Canadian teams. In 1925, the Boston Bruins became the first American team in the league. As the league expanded into American cities, Canadian fans felt their sport was being taken over by American economic interests. The integration of the American and Canadian economies through the league's expansion frustrated those who saw hockey as distinctly Canadian.

Canadians also resented American involvement in Vietnam and felt their sovereignty was eroding due to NATO and NORAD obligations. At the height of tensions with their southern neighbors, many Canadians considered US cultural and economic dominance a greater threat to Canadian independence than the military threat posed by the Soviet Union. They blamed their national team's lack of Cold War success on America — the best Canadians were professionals, ineligible under IIHF rules, because the NHL refused to release their players.

When Pierre Trudeau became Prime Minister in 1968, he pursued a policy of distancing Canada from the United States. Under Trudeau, Canada became the first NATO country to officially interact with the Soviet Union after the invasion of Czechoslovakia.

1972 Summit Series

Canada vs. The Soviet Union

The 1972 Summit Series was one of Trudeau's efforts to strengthen Canada's diplomatic relationship with the Soviet Union independently of the United States. The eight-game series would be the first matchup between Canadian NHLers and the Soviet national team — and was supposed to definitively prove that Canada was the world's best hockey country.

In the leadup, Canadians were heavily favored. The Soviets shocked Canada with a 7–3 victory in the first game at Montreal Forum. The Soviets won the next three games held in Canada. While the Canadian team tied the fourth game and came from behind to win the last three in Moscow, the early losses at home were a profound reality check.

"This game burst a few balloons in our team's thinking. There isn't any doubt that we were outplayed and out checked. It was a good game and we don't have much to be ashamed about, but it proved to our guys that the Russians can't be taken lightly."

— Alan Eagleson, The Globe and Mail, 1972

The series forced Canadian fans expecting a showcase of NHL superiority to reckon with the strength of the Soviet program. But it also produced something unexpected: admiration. Canadians found themselves impressed by the discipline, conditioning, and skill of Soviet players. Hockey became "socialism with a human face," and Soviet players were viewed not as a collective representation of a hostile ideology, but as athletes who had earned respect on the ice.

The series generated genuine goodwill between the two countries, and led to a physical cultural exchange — 3,000 Canadian fans traveled to Moscow to watch the away games.

1976 Super Series

The Broad Street Bullies vs. CSKA Moscow

Sometimes, the aggression of Canadian hockey undermined the country's diplomatic efforts. One such example is the 1976 Super Series matchup between the Philadelphia Flyers — who had a predominantly Canadian roster — and CSKA Moscow.

The two teams could not have been more different. The Flyers earned the nickname "the Broad Street Bullies" in the early 1970s due to their effective but aggressive style of play, while CSKA Moscow was known for speed and precise passing. The game was violent from the onset. After Flyers defenseman Ed Van Impe leveled Soviet star Valeri Kharlamov with a hard hit in the first period — and no penalty was called — the Soviets nearly quit in protest. Van Impe later stated that he'd "just wanted to welcome him to Philadelphia."

The Score

The Flyers won 4–1 when the Soviet team returned to the ice. It proved that physical, aggressive hockey was effective against Soviet finesse — but at the cost of diplomatic goodwill.

The Contrast

The game illustrated the two styles in sharp relief: the Soviets' fluid, collective game versus the Flyers' physical intimidation — and the irony that Canada's most "Canadian" team damaged Canada's Soviet relationship.

1976 Canada Cup

Canada, America, and Reconciliation

Canada's relationship with the United States was also heavily influenced by hockey. When Gerald Ford became president in 1974, it marked a period of improved relations between Canada and the United States — due to his good personal relationship with Trudeau and a shift towards a less confrontational diplomatic approach.

Under Ford, the United States competed in the 1976 Canada Cup alongside Canada, the Soviet Union, Czechoslovakia, Sweden, and Finland. The Cup was scheduled in September before the start of most professional leagues, so that each country could use its best players. Canada defeated Czechoslovakia to win the tournament, and the Americans — fielding a team of mediocre NHL players and minor leaguers — surprisingly tied the Czechoslovaks and beat the Finns. Several games were played in American cities as a sign of goodwill.

The AHAUS had been hesitant to send a team, fearing an embarrassing performance. Instead, the tournament offered a moment of unexpected competitiveness — and, for Canada and America, a measure of reconciliation through the sport that had long divided them.

Connection to Thesis

Across the Summit Series, the Super Series, and the Canada Cup, Canada used hockey not as a proxy for American Cold War policy, but as an instrument of its own national identity and diplomacy. The pattern mirrors what happened within the Eastern Bloc: the lines drawn by superpower rivalries were constantly crossed, complicated, and redrawn — on the ice, in the stands, and in the relations between nations that were supposed to be on the same side.
