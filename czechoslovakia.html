Czechoslovakia

Taping Over the Red Star

How a nation suppressed by tanks found its voice on the ice — and how a hockey victory nearly became a revolution.

A Hockey Nation Before It Was a Nation

Bohemia and the Origins of Czech Hockey

Bohemia was one of the first countries in Europe to play ice hockey — and one of the founding members of the IIHF in 1908. The Habsburgs were concerned that Bohemia's membership in international sports organizations was fostering separatism. While they managed to have the kingdom removed from FIFA and the IOC, Bohemia remained a member of the IIHF until the Czech lands were combined with Slovakia after the collapse of the Austro-Hungarian Empire.

At the beginning of World War II, Czechoslovakia sided with the Allies, but in the 1938 Munich Agreement the allies gave the Sudetenland to Germany without Czechoslovak approval. Germany subsequently invaded in 1939. This series of events left Czechoslovaks feeling betrayed by the West, and led them to negotiate with Stalin — paving the way for the 1948 communist coup that turned Czechoslovakia into a Soviet satellite state.

After World War II, the Czechoslovak hockey team was very successful, winning its first World Championship in 1947, finishing second in 1948, and winning again in 1949.

Hockey Under the Red Star

Oppression of a National Pastime

Shortly after the communist dictatorship took control, it began systematically oppressing the hockey program that had been a source of national pride.

In 1950, the national team had been preparing to travel to London for the World Championships when they were prevented from boarding the plane, allegedly due to a visa dispute involving journalists. When angry players gathered at a bar and complained about the communist government, they were arrested and imprisoned for "conspiracy and treason." Several Czechoslovak players speculated that they were imprisoned in order to make way for the Soviet hockey team.

State Control of Audiences

Officials controlled ticket distribution for games against international teams to prevent unruly behavior from revealing bloc tensions to visiting reporters. Exhibition games were moved from Prague to smaller cities to reduce the risk of riots in the capital.

Media Framing

State media framed both Czechoslovak and Soviet victories as evidence of the superiority of the communist system — rather than as competitions between rival teams — blurring the distinction between ally and subject.

Players also resisted individually. Many Czechoslovak players defected to the West despite having to leave their families behind and face imprisonment if they failed. Those who remained found other forms of protest: taping over the red star on their jerseys, refusing to shake hands with Soviet players, and starting fights that the Soviet press was forced to describe as "friendly matches among friends" to maintain the appearance of unity.

In one notable example, a non-league game in which two formerly imprisoned members of the 1950 national team were scheduled to play drew three times the expected crowd. Officials were scared enough to bar the former prisoners from participating — which itself triggered a small riot.

1968

Prague Spring and Its End

Prague Spring was a period of political reform that began when Alexander Dubček became the leader of the Czechoslovak communist party in 1968. He attempted to pass democratizing reforms such as loosening control on the economy, allowing freedom of expression, and ending press censorship.

The Soviet Union feared these reforms would set a bad example for other Eastern Bloc countries and shift Czechoslovak foreign policy away from the Warsaw Pact. The period of reform ended when the Soviet Union led thousands of Warsaw Pact troops and tanks into Czechoslovakia in August 1968.

World Championships, 1969

The Hockey Riots

The 1969 World Championships were supposed to be held in Prague but were moved to Stockholm following the Soviet invasion. The tournament format was a double round robin, where the Czechoslovaks won 2 games against the Soviets — but lost to Sweden and ended up in third place.

"The Czechoslovak team fulfilled the country's greatest sporting dream — to wrench the world hockey crown from the Russians. Czechoslovakia had not won the title since 1949."

— The Globe and Mail, 1972

During the second game against the Soviets, Czechoslovak fans held up anti-Soviet posters. Czechoslovak players taped over the five-point star on their jerseys and refused to shake hands with Soviet players after the match.

After the game, more than 100,000 people participated in protests in Prague alone, with similar events across the country. Protests occurred outside Soviet headquarters, embassies, barracks, and military hospitals. Windows were broken, military vehicles were set on fire, and protestors resisted police and Soviet troops who tried to restore order.

What Happened at the Aeroflot Office? — A Historiographical Debate

The destruction of the Prague office of Soviet airline Aeroflot became a justification for the crackdown — known as normalization — that followed the riots. Two historians interpret the evidence differently.

John Soares

Possible Soviet Setup

Soares notes several suspicious details: the participants were grown men throwing paving stones rather than students, and the police did not intervene. He suggests the destruction may have been orchestrated by Soviet state security as a pretext to remove Dubček, who had remained in his position for seven months after the invasion.

Ondřej Tůma

Genuine Grassroots Protest

Tůma responds directly to Soares' argument but argues the evidence is not definitive. Constant construction in Prague made paving stones unremarkable, protests were not limited to students, and similar acts of destruction occurred elsewhere in the country.

Both interpretations agree that the outcome of the riots served Soviet rhetoric. After Dubček was replaced with hardliner Gustáv Husák, the political situation in Czechoslovakia was brought under control, and sport became one of the last accessible forms of protest. The Czechoslovak government continued to take special security measures around Soviet-Czechoslovak matches until 1989.

Connection to Thesis

Czechoslovaks used hockey to express their discontent with Soviet control in ways that no other form of public life permitted. The red star taped over on a jersey, the refused handshake, the riots that spread from a tournament in Stockholm to the streets of Prague — all of these demonstrate that Cold War hockey tensions were not simply a product of East/West ideological conflict. They ran deep within the blocs themselves.
