Ice Hockey and the Cold War

Four nations. One game. A conflict fought not only in secret meetings and arms races, but on frozen rinks from Prague to Lake Placid.

Thesis While American popular culture focuses on the U.S./Soviet rivalry in Cold War ice hockey, international competitions during this period were equally defined by tensions within the blocs. Players and fans repeatedly acted in ways that defied their country's official stance in the Cold War: adversaries formed friendships and cheered for one another, and allied countries had incredibly tense matches.
Context

During the Cold War, sports competitions became increasingly politicized, because the nuclear age forced states — specifically the United States and the Soviet Union — to find non-military ways of asserting the superiority of their ideological systems. Ice hockey competitions became especially tense. Almost all Warsaw Pact and NATO countries, as well as all European neutrals, played in at least one Olympic hockey tournament.

The sport was especially significant for four key countries. For the US and the Soviet Union, the structure of their hockey programs and style of play directly reflected their ideological systems. For Czechoslovakia and Canada, hockey was an important part of their national identities. Because Czechoslovakia and Canada were significantly weaker than their respective superpower allies, hockey allowed them to express their independence and pursue their own goals.

History of International Ice Hockey

Two Eras of Dominance

The International Ice Hockey Federation (IIHF), which is the governing body of international ice hockey to this day, was founded in Paris in 1908 as the Ligue Internationale de Hockey sur Glace (LIHG). The IIHF recognizes the first World Championship as the 1920 Olympics in Belgium, and the first World Championship outside the Olympics as the 1930 tournament in Germany.

International ice hockey during the 20th century can be divided into two periods: Canadian and Soviet dominance. Between 1920–1952, Canada won gold in 6 out of 7 Olympic Games and 15 out of 18 World Championships. Between 1954–1988, the Soviet Union won gold in 7 out of 9 Olympics and 21 out of 34 World Championships — finishing every Olympic and World Championship with a medal.
